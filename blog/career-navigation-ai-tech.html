Navigating a Career in AI and Technology: Clarity Over Credentials

26 May 2025

Career paths in AI and technology are rarely the straight lines that retrospective accounts make them appear. Most people working effectively in this space followed routes that were partially improvised — shaped by opportunity, curiosity, luck, and a few deliberate choices made with incomplete information. The recognition that non-linearity is normal, rather than a sign of poor planning, is itself a useful starting point. What follows is a framework for navigating that non-linearity with more clarity and less anxiety.

The Problem with Credential-Chasing

The instinct to accumulate credentials is understandable. Credentials are legible: they signal competence to people who do not know you, and they provide a sense of forward motion during periods of uncertainty. But credential-chasing has a distinctive failure mode — it optimises for appearances of capability rather than capability itself, and it tends to substitute activity for direction.

The more fundamental question to ask before acquiring any new credential is: what specific opportunity or role does this open up for me, and is that where I actually want to go? If the honest answer is "I am not sure, but it seems like the right thing to do", that is a signal to pause. Credentials acquired without a clear purpose tend to sit on a CV and not much else.

Identifying Your Sustainable Edge

A sustainable career position is one that is genuinely distinctive — that reflects a combination of strengths that is unusual and difficult to replicate at scale. In AI and technology, the obvious differentiator is technical depth, and it matters. But it is rarely sufficient on its own. The professionals who build the most resilient careers tend to combine technical competence with at least one other dimension:

  • Communication: The ability to explain technical concepts to non-technical audiences accurately and clearly is genuinely scarce, and its value compounds as you move into senior or advisory roles.
  • Domain expertise: Deep knowledge of a specific industry or application area — healthcare, financial services, manufacturing, education — makes technical skills far more deployable. Domain experts who are also technically literate are rare and valuable.
  • Cross-cultural range: The ability to work effectively across different national, organisational, and professional cultures is an increasingly important edge in a field that operates globally and rarely respects organisational or national boundaries.

Identifying your own version of this combination — what are you better at than most people, and what do you care enough about to keep developing? — is one of the most useful investments you can make in your career direction.

The Value of T-Shaped Competencies

The T-shaped professional is a useful model: deep expertise in one area (the vertical bar), combined with sufficient breadth to collaborate effectively across adjacent domains (the horizontal bar). In AI and technology, the vertical bar might be machine learning engineering, data architecture, natural language processing, or policy and governance. The horizontal bar might include product thinking, research methods, stakeholder management, or communication.

The T shape is more robust than either pure depth or pure breadth. Pure depth risks obsolescence as specific technologies shift; pure breadth risks irrelevance as the bar for substantive contribution rises. The combination — real expertise in one area, plus the range to work across others — tends to age well.

Building a Public Body of Work

In a field where credentialing is imperfect and hiring processes are often opaque, a visible body of work provides a different kind of signal. Writing that demonstrates how you think, open-source contributions that show how you build, participation in standards or community processes that reveal your engagement with the field — these accumulate into something that a CV does not easily capture.

"Your reputation in a field is built less by what you claim to know and more by what you visibly do with what you know."

This does not require prolific output. A handful of pieces of writing that are genuinely clear and substantive, or a modest open-source contribution that solves a real problem, is worth more than a high-volume but shallow presence. The goal is to give people who do not know you something concrete to engage with.

When to Specialise vs. Stay General

The question of when to specialise and when to stay general does not have a universal answer — but it does have a personal one. Early in a career, breadth tends to pay better returns: it builds the contextual knowledge that makes depth more valuable later, and it keeps optionality open. As a career develops, deliberately deepening in areas where you have genuine interest and some natural advantage produces compounding returns that generalism cannot match.

The practical test: if you imagine yourself doing a particular kind of work five years from now, does that prospect feel energising or merely acceptable? Sustainable specialisation usually requires some genuine interest in the domain — not necessarily passion, but enough curiosity to sustain continued learning and engagement over time.

Navigating International and Multicultural Environments

AI and technology are global fields, and careers in them increasingly involve working across different national contexts, organisational cultures, and professional norms. The ability to navigate these differences — to communicate clearly across them, to avoid assuming your own norms are universal, and to bring genuine curiosity to unfamiliar contexts — is a practical skill with real career value. It is also one that develops through experience rather than instruction, which means deliberately seeking diverse working environments rather than staying in familiar ones.

Practical Steps: Mentorship, Peer Groups, and Career Reviews

The structural practices that support effective career navigation are simpler than most people expect. A mentor who has navigated a similar path — and who is willing to be honest rather than merely encouraging — provides perspective that is hard to replicate. Peer groups of people at a similar career stage, who are willing to share what is actually working and what is not, provide accountability and pattern recognition across different trajectories. And a regular career review — quarterly or semi-annual, asking what is working, what is not, and what the next significant choice is — provides the orientation to make deliberate moves rather than reactive ones. None of this is complicated. The barrier is prioritising it over the urgent day-to-day.
