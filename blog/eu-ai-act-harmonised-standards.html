The EU AI Act and Harmonised Standards: Where Compliance Will Actually Be Defined

29 June 2026

The EU AI Act is the most consequential piece of AI regulation in force anywhere, and most organisations within its scope have by now read summaries of its risk tiers and obligations. But the Act itself is only half of the compliance picture. Like most EU product legislation, it states obligations at the level of legal principle (risk management shall be established, data governance shall be applied, accuracy and robustness shall be ensured) and delegates the technical detail of how to a second layer: harmonised European standards. For high-risk AI systems, that second layer is where compliance will actually be defined. Organisations that track only the regulation are watching half the game.

The New Legislative Framework Logic

The AI Act follows the EU's long-established "New Legislative Framework" approach, familiar from domains such as machinery, medical devices, and toys. The legislation sets essential requirements; European standardisation organisations develop harmonised standards that specify how to meet them; and once a standard is cited in the Official Journal of the EU, conformity with it grants a presumption of conformity with the corresponding legal requirements.

That presumption is the crucial mechanism. Harmonised standards remain formally voluntary (a provider can always demonstrate compliance by other means), but in practice, following the harmonised standard is by far the cheapest and most defensible route. The alternative is convincing an assessor, from first principles, that your bespoke approach satisfies the law. For most organisations that is not a serious option, which is why harmonised standards function as de facto mandatory technical rules.

Enter CEN/CENELEC JTC 21

The body drafting these standards is CEN/CENELEC Joint Technical Committee 21 (JTC 21), working under a standardisation request issued by the European Commission. The request covers the core themes of the Act's high-risk requirements: risk management, data governance and quality, record keeping and logging, transparency and information to users, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity, quality management, and conformity assessment.

JTC 21's working method matters for anyone trying to anticipate the outcome. Wherever possible, it builds on international work, adopting or adapting ISO/IEC standards developed in JTC 1/SC42, and drafts "homegrown" European standards only where the international catalogue does not meet the Act's specific needs. This is why organisations engaging with SC42 standards such as ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management systems) and ISO/IEC 23894 (AI risk management) are not doing something separate from EU compliance: they are engaging with the raw material from which much of the European framework is being built. The fit is not one-to-one, since European standards must track the Act's legal requirements, including its fundamental-rights dimension, more tightly than international texts do, but the direction of travel is unmistakable.

"Harmonised standards are formally voluntary. In practice, they are where the meaning of 'compliant' gets written, clause by clause, in technical committees most affected organisations have never heard of."

The Timing Problem

The uncomfortable reality is that the standards layer has lagged the legal layer. The Act entered into force in August 2024 with obligations applying in phases: prohibitions first, then general-purpose AI obligations, then the high-risk requirements. Developing consensus-based technical standards is slow, and the timeline for delivering and citing the full set of harmonised standards has been under visible strain, with deadlines extended and the broader implementation calendar itself the subject of continued political negotiation in Brussels.

For providers of high-risk systems, this creates a planning dilemma rather than an excuse. Waiting for final, cited standards before starting compliance work compresses all the effort into the least time. The more robust strategy is to build now on the most stable available material (the Act's own essential requirements, the mature SC42 standards that JTC 21 is drawing on, and the published drafts of the European work) and to treat final harmonised standards as a confirmation and adjustment step rather than a starting gun.

What This Means in Practice

For organisations developing or deploying AI systems that may fall in the high-risk category, a few working conclusions follow:

  • Classify early, and document the reasoning. Everything downstream depends on whether a system is high-risk under the Act. This analysis is worth doing rigorously and revisiting as guidance evolves.
  • Track the standards layer, not just the regulation. Legal summaries of the Act are abundant; visibility into JTC 21 and SC42 work is scarcer and more valuable. The technical detail of your future obligations is being drafted there now.
  • Use the international standards as scaffolding. An AI management system aligned with ISO/IEC 42001 and risk management aligned with ISO/IEC 23894 will not automatically equal AI Act conformity, but it builds the organisational machinery that conformity will require, and it does so against stable, published texts.
  • Design documentation for conformity assessment from the start. Technical documentation, logging, and quality management are far cheaper to build into a system than to retrofit under deadline pressure.
  • Consider participating. Standards committees are open through national standards bodies. Organisations with a significant stake in how requirements are operationalised can influence the text rather than merely receiving it, and even observer-level participation yields months of advance visibility.

The Strategic View

It is tempting to treat all of this as a European compliance problem to be handled by a legal team. That underestimates what is happening. The combination of the AI Act and its harmonised standards is producing the first comprehensive, operational definition of what "trustworthy AI" means in engineering and organisational terms, and, as with GDPR before it, that definition will radiate well beyond the EU through procurement, market access, and regulatory imitation. The organisations that internalise the standards layer early are not just reducing compliance risk; they are learning the vocabulary in which AI quality will be specified globally for the next decade.
